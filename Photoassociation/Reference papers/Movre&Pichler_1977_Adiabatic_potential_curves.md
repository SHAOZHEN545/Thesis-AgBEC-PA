# Resonance interaction and self-broadening of alkali resonance lines. I. Adiabatic potential curves

**Mladen Movre and Goran Pichler**
Institute of Physics of the University, Zagreb, POB 304, Yugoslavia

Received 29 June 1976, in final form 28 March 1977

## Abstract
Adiabatic potential curves were calculated using perturbation theory for the long-range electrostatic interaction between two alkali atoms of the same species, one being in the ground state, the other in the resonance-excited state. Comparison with recent experiments showed that the observed satellites and asymmetries in the self-broadened quasi-static wings of the alkali resonance lines are the consequence of extrema in certain potential curves between fine-structure levels.

## 1. Introduction

Recent absorption measurements by Niemax and Pichler (1974, 1975) of the self-broadened quasi-static wings of the alkali resonance lines have pointed to the need for detailed calculations of the interaction energies between two atoms of the same species, one being in the ground state and the other in the first excited state. These long-range resonance interactions have been calculated in uncoupled (Fontana 1961) and coupled representations (Fontana 1962) using perturbation theory. The uncoupled representation represents the case when the electrostatic interaction between atoms is much stronger than the spin-orbit interaction within the atoms, while the coupled representation represents the case when the electrostatic interaction between the atoms is much weaker than the spin-orbit interaction within the atoms. Unfortunately, none of these approximations can explain the observed satellites and asymmetries (Ch'en 1940, Gregory 1942, Niemax and Pichler 1974, 1975), since these phenomena evidently fall into the intermediate case, where spin-orbit and electrostatic interactions are of comparable size. However, the relevant potential curves were treated theoretically by Dashevskaya et al (1969) in connection with the excitation transfer in collisions between identical alkali atoms. Unfortunately, this was not known to the previously mentioned authors. We have recalculated these potential curves in order to show explicitly that satellites and asymmetries found in experiments (Niemax and Pichler 1974, 1975) are due to the extrema in the potential curves (pseudocrossings). The calculations of the relevant molecular oscillator strengths will be presented in a following paper together with a further and more detailed comparison with the measured profiles.

## 2. Theory

In an alkali atomic vapour the interaction between close atoms leads to the formation of the short-lived molecular system. In order to find the resonance interaction energies between normal and excited atoms we used the perturbation method. Essentially it is the Heitler-London approach to molecular structure calculations, but all overlapping integrals in the long-range approximation make a negligible contribution to the interaction energies. The first excited doublet levels $^2P_{1/2}$ and $^2P_{3/2}$ in all alkali atoms are comparatively close to each other but well isolated from all other levels in the term diagram. In the range of the interatomic distances, where the conditions for binary and non-overlapping collisions are satisfied, the resonance interaction is comparable to the spin-orbit interaction, but it is always smaller than the separation of the first excited doublet from the other levels. Therefore it is permissible to take the finite set of the basis wavefunctions for the diagonalisation of the Hamiltonian of the long-range molecular system.

### 2.1. Adiabatic potential curves

The molecular states resulting from the atomic states $(^2P_J + {}^2S_{1/2})$ may be classified in the adiabatic approximation according to certain symmetry properties (Wigner and Witmer 1928, Herzberg 1953). The designation of the molecular terms is generally of the form $\Omega_\sigma^{(\pm)}$, where $\Omega=|M|$ is the absolute value of the projection of total electronic angular momentum along the molecular axis, $\sigma=g$ or $u$ is the parity of term, and superscript $\pm$ is the point group character for the reflection of the term $\Omega=0$ in the plane containing the molecular axis. The secular determinant of $24\times24$ type can be factorised into subdeterminants of the first, second and third order due to the fact that states with different $M$ do not combine. The use of the dimensionless quantities

$$
X = \frac{C}{9R^3\Delta}
\tag{1}
$$

and

$$
Y = \frac{E - E_{1/2}^0}{\Delta}
\tag{2}
$$

where $C$ is equal to $e^2|\langle n0|r|nl\rangle|^2$ i.e. the square of the radial part of the atomic dipole matrix element, $\Delta$ the fine-structure splitting $E_{3/2}^0 - E_{1/2}^0$, $R$ the internuclear separation, and $E$ the energy of the system, makes it possible to write the equations for the adiabatic potentials in a uniform way for all alkali homonuclear pairs. Thus we obtain the following secular equations:

for $2_\sigma$ states,
$$
Y = 1 + \sigma 3X
\tag{3}
$$

for $1_\sigma$ states,
$$
Y^3 + (-2 + \sigma 6X)Y^2 + (1 - \sigma 8X - 9X^2)Y + (\sigma 2X + 6X^2 - \sigma 54X^3) = 0
\tag{4}
$$

for $0_\sigma^+$ states,
$$
Y^2 - (1 + \sigma 9X)Y + (\sigma 4X + 18X^2) = 0
\tag{5}
$$

and for $0_\sigma^-$ states,
$$
Y^2 - (1 - \sigma 3X)Y - 18X^2 = 0
\tag{6}
$$

In equations (3)-(6) $\sigma$ equals $+1$ for gerade states, and $-1$ for ungerade states. The representation of the adiabatic potentials in the form of algebraic equations (3)-(6) is very convenient, since they can be solved either in $X$ or in $Y$. Furthermore, they may be used for an easy evaluation of any derivative, once $X$ and $Y$ are given. The results of the calculations in the $(X, Y)$ coordinates are shown in figures 1 and 2.

In the intermediate region some interesting extrema occur. Thus the $1_u$ potential curve emerging from the $(^2P_{1/2} + {}^2S_{1/2})$ level has a maximum, the lower $1_u$ potential curve emerging from the $(^2P_{3/2} + {}^2S_{1/2})$ level has very shallow minimum, and the $0_g^-$ potential curve emerging from the same level has a minimum. The corresponding $X$ and $Y$ values are given in table 1.

Three pairs of the adiabatic potentials ($0_g^-$ and $0_u^-$, $0_g^+$ and $2_u$, $0_u^+$ and $2_g$) which are degenerate in the coupled approximation, diverge in the intermediate region and converge on different levels of the uncoupled approximation region. In the model proposed by Niemax and Pichler (1975) it was suggested that two $0_u^-$ potential curves repel each other forming one minimum and one maximum (the avoided crossing) which are responsible for the satellites. This idea is essentially correct, but in fact there is repulsion between the two $1_u$ states and between the two $0_g^-$ states. The latter pseudocrossing realises only one extremum since in the coupled approximation the lower $0_g^-$ potential curve has a zero splitting.

The notation $\Omega_\sigma^{(\pm)}$ adopted for the coupled approximation retains its meaning both in the intermediate and in the uncoupled approximation regions. In the latter case the total spin $S$ and the projection $\Lambda$ of the total orbital angular momentum on the internuclear axis become conserved and the $^{2S+1}\Lambda_\sigma^{(\pm)}$ notation is more appropriate. In the uncoupled approximation the $+$ and $-$ superscripts denote the symmetry properties of the coordinate wavefunctions, whereas in the coupled approximation they relate to the whole wavefunction of the molecular system.

## 3. Comparison of theoretical and experimental results

For comparison with experimental results it is now necessary to transform the universal potential curves in the $(X, Y)$ representation into the usual $(R, U(R))$ representation. For this, according to equations (1) and (2), we need the fine-structure splitting of the resonance levels and squares of the radial part of the dipole matrix elements. We used the fine-structure splitting of the alkali atoms from the tables of Moore (1949, 1952, 1958) which are reproduced in table 3. Instead of using theoretical values for the radial matrix elements, we deduced them from the experimental oscillator strengths by means of the following relation

$$
|\langle n0|r|nl\rangle|^2 = \frac{3h}{4\pi mc(E_1 - E_0)} \times \begin{cases} \frac{3}{2}f_{3/2} \\ 3f_{1/2}. \end{cases}
\tag{14}
$$

In the case of the heavier alkali atoms the radial part of the dipole matrix elements differs for the fine-structure components. Since we are discussing predominantly the potential curves in the intermediate region, it is more convenient to use the mean values of the relevant matrix elements, as given in table 2.

In order to compare the results of our present calculations with the experimental observations of Niemax and Pichler (1975) it may be useful to reproduce briefly the main features of the far wings of the measured alkali resonance lines. All lines had symmetric Lorentzian wings up to the point at which asymmetry begins. The second and third columns of table 4 show the wavelength distances from the line centres where asymmetries start. The stronger components of the Na, K, Rb and Cs resonance lines have blue asymmetry (the larger absorption coefficient in the blue wing), while at the beginning of the asymmetry a broad satellite was found in the red wing in the cases of Rb and Cs. The weaker components, however, exhibit red asymmetry, while in the cases of Rb and Cs on the blue wing at the beginning of the asymmetry a broad satellite was found. The fourth and fifth columns of table 4 show the positions of the maximum of $1_u(1/2)$ and the minimum of $0_g^-(3/2)$ (in terms of $\text{\AA}$ units), which fit the experimental data fairly well. From table 4 one can see that the calculated extrema are always situated farther from the line centres than are the beginnings of the asymmetry.

## 4. Discussion

In this section we shall discuss the approximations involved in present calculations. We confine ourselves mostly to the region of the extrema in the potential curves because of their connection with observed satellites.

We have supposed that overlapping integrals can be neglected. From the analysis of the exchange interaction in excited states by Dashevskaya et al (1969) it appears that their contribution in the region of extrema is negligible. In the case of the first excited states of $\text{Rb}_2$, the calculations of Reznikov and Umansky (1971) for the intermediate internuclear separations ($2 < R < 5\text{\AA}$) showed that exchange and resonance interactions are equal at $5\text{\AA}$. From the exponential behaviour of the exchange interaction one concludes that it gives a negligible contribution at $18\text{\AA}$ where the Rb satellites are situated.

Dickinson and Rudge (1970) have calculated the semi-theoretical potential energy curves for diatomic alkali molecules in the ground states. They also find that exchange effects appear at internuclear distances much smaller than those where the extrema occur. One can roughly estimate the internuclear separations at which the exchange effects are dominant by summing up the approximate radii of the normal and excited atoms. By means of the formula

$$
r_e = \frac{1}{2}a_0 n^* [(5n^*)^2 + 1 - 3l(l+1)]^{1/2}
\tag{15}
$$

where $a_0$ is the Bohr radius, $n^*$ the effective principal quantum number, and $l$ the orbital quantum number, we obtained for the lower limits the values 4.9, 5.6, 6.4, 6.7 and 7.2 $\text{\AA}$ for Li, Na, K, Rb and Cs, respectively. Comparing these values with the last three columns of table 3, one can see that the calculated extrema fall into the region where our potential curves are reliable.

The present calculations were carried out only in the first order. The second-order effects should be taken into account for both ground and excited levels. It appears that the difference of these van der Waals energies for the excited and ground level will move singularities slightly towards longer wavelengths. The effect is largest in the case of Cs, where we estimated a correction of about $6\text{\AA}$.

If we now want to apply the calculated interaction potentials in the interpretation of the quasi-static wings of the alkali resonance lines, we have to discuss the validity criterion for the quasi-static approximation and the adiabaticity of our potential curves. We are not going to discuss the whole problem in detail, but rather we shall confine ourselves to the observability of the satellites. From figure 1 we can see that in the case of all extrema, their separations from the line centre is always smaller than the distances to the nearest potential curves. From this we may conclude that the validity criterion for the quasi-static approximation will be a stronger condition for the observability of the satellites than is the criterion for the adiabatic approximation. Only in the case of the minimum in $0_g^-$ do both seem to be of equal importance. Since the quasi-static approximation is not valid in an extremum, we are here only discussing the potential in its neighbourhood. Rough estimations for alkali vapours at a temperature of approximately $300^\circ\text{C}$ show that the minimum in $0_g^-$ and the maximum in $1_u$ for Rb and Cs actually satisfy both conditions for observability. All other satellites of Na, K, Rb and Cs fall into the region where the quasi-static approximation ceases to be valid, and are smeared out.

## 5. Conclusion

In this paper we have calculated the adiabatic potential curves for the resonance interaction between two alkali atoms of the same species. The splitting structure of the interaction potentials, which are valid only in the long-range region, is very complex. Three potential curves have their extrema in the intermediate-coupling region. Two of these extrema are responsible for the satellites which were experimentally observed in the far wings of the Rb and Cs resonance lines (Niemax and Pichler 1975).

The interaction potential curves are the first step in the construction of the theoretical quasi-static wing profile of the alkali resonance lines.
