# Photoassociative Spectroscopy of $1_{g}$, $0_{u}^{+}$, and $0_{g}^{-}$ States of $Na_{2}$

**L. P. Ratliff, M. E. Wagshul, P. D. Lett, S. L. Rolston, and W. D. Phillips** *Atomic Physics Division, Phys A-167, National Institute of Standards and Technology, Gaithersburg, Maryland 20899* (Received 21 February 1994; accepted 27 May 1994)

DOI: [https://doi.org/10.1063/1.467638](https://doi.org/10.1063/1.467638)

***

## Abstract
We use photoassociation of ultracold Na to study transitions from free atoms to bound molecules. We obtain rovibrational spectra of the $1_{g}$, the $0_{u}^{+}$, and, for the first time, the $0_{g}^{-}$ "purely long-range" state of $Na_{2}$, all of which dissociate to $Na(3^{2}S_{1/2}) + Na(3^{2}P_{3/2})$.

***

Recently, photoassociation of laser cooled atoms¹ has been used to perform spectroscopy of alkali dimers.²⁻⁵ This technique probes free-bound transitions (from free atoms to a bound molecule) with high resolution. Unprecedented resolution is achieved with ultracold atoms (<1 mK) because their kinetic energy spread is on the order of the atomic natural linewidth and the Doppler broadening is much less. Because the atom pairs can be excited at small relative velocity and extremely large internuclear separation, molecular states very near the dissociation limit are accessible.

Here, we report spectroscopy of the $1_{g}$, $0_{u}^{+}$, and $0_{g}^{-}$ states of $Na_{2}$ that are asymptotically $Na(3^{2}S_{1/2}) + Na(3^{2}P_{3/2})$. The $1_{g}$ spectrum, investigated over a range of 170 ${cm}^{-1}$, exhibits the change from predominantly Hund's case (c) near dissociation, to predominantly Hund's case (a) in more deeply bound states. In the pure Hund's case (a) limit, the optical transition to the $1_{g}(^{1}\Pi_{g})$ state is forbidden by dipole selection rules; nevertheless, we obtain an excellent signal-to-noise ratio. The $0_{g}^{-}$ state of $Na_{2}$ is a "purely long-range" state⁶,⁷ (inner turning point at $\sim 55\ a_{0}$) and its spectroscopy has not been reported previously.

Our photoassociative spectroscopy technique involves illuminating a sample of laser cooled and trapped atoms with an independent, tunable (probe) laser. The trapping/cooling laser and the probe laser are alternated at 50 kHz. Ions are produced when a photon resonantly excites a free-bound transition to an excited molecule and a second photon ionizes the molecule (see Fig. 1). The structure of the ionization spectrum is due to the first step. Autoionizing resonances in the bound-free continuum of the second step are broad enough that they will not alter the line shapes or positions of structure from the first step, although they may affect the relative intensities of separated lines.

The ions are collected, and the signal is gated into two counters corresponding to "trap-phase" and "probe-phase" ion signals. Because the trap laser frequency is constant, the trap-phase ion signal is a measure of the number of trapped atoms. This number is constant except when the probe laser is resonant with a free-bound transition to an excited molecular state, which is ionized or decays into either a stable molecule or a pair of energetic free atoms (see Fig. 1). This results in a loss of atoms from the trap. Thus, we obtain two spectra of molecular excitation as a function of probe laser frequency: one by photoionization of excited state molecules, during the probe phase, and the other by trap loss due to the formation of excited state molecules, detected during the trap phase.

We use a "dark spot" magneto-optical trap (MOT) to collect Na atoms in the $3^{2}S_{1/2}(F=1)$ state at high density and low temperature.¹⁰ This trap is similar to the MOT we used previously except that, in the central portion of the trap, the atoms are optically pumped into the "dark" $F=1$ ground state where they no longer interact with the light; this reduces the rate of excited state processes that limit trap density. This technique has produced¹⁰ densities of $\sim 10^{12}{cm}^{-3}$. We have not accurately measured our density, but we found that the "dark spot" MOT increased our probe-phase signal one hundredfold over that in our standard MOT.

Figure 2 shows a probe-phase spectrum which extends over 170 ${cm}^{-1}$; the vibrational series that we identify as belonging to the $1_{g}(^{1}\Pi_{g})$ state [which dissociates to $Na(3^{2}S_{1/2}) + Na(3^{2}P_{3/2})$ at 16 973 ${cm}^{-1}$] spans the full range. Below 16 900 ${cm}^{-1}$, the modulation in intensity of the vibrational peaks is mainly due to the Franck-Condon factors for the first excitation which are predicted to have minima at 16 800 and 16 875 ${cm}^{-1}$. Near 16 956 ${cm}^{-1}$ we see a dense, complicated spectrum, as yet unassigned, associated with the $3^{2}S_{1/2} + 3^{2}P_{1/2}$ dissociation limit. Just above 16 956 and 16 973 ${cm}^{-1}$ are large peaks associated with atomic ground state hyperfine structure.¹²

Resonances appearing only in the trap-phase signal are due to transitions to excited molecular states that do not tend to ionize but do cause trap loss.

Of the five attractive potentials that become $3^{2}S_{1/2} + 3^{2}P_{3/2}$ asymptotically, we have identified all except the $1_{u}$, which is less than 0.2 ${cm}^{-1}$ deep, and the $2_{u}$ which is only radiatively coupled to the ground state at extremely long range. We have identified the $1_{g}$, $0_{u}^{+}$, and $0_{g}^{-}$ states based on (1) the energy range over which they are seen, (2) their vibrational spacing, (3) their rotational spacing, (4) their hyperfine structure, and (5) the relative sizes of their trap-phase and probe-phase signals.

The $1_{g}$ and $0_{u}^{+}$ series appear in both probe-phase and trap-phase signals and have similar vibrational spacings. The series labeled $1_{g}$ in Fig. 3 extends the entire range in Fig. 2, while that labeled $0_{u}^{+}$ is unambiguously identified only within 1.5 ${cm}^{-1}$ of dissociation. Although we extracted $C_{3}$ coefficients for these series (from the $V = C_{3}/R^{3}$ long range portion of the potentials, as done previously), we cannot distinguish between the series based on these coefficients because the $1_{g}$ and $0_{u}^{+}$ potential curves are nearly identical at long range. We can, however, assign these states based on the hyperfine structure (see Fig. 3) since the $1_{g}$ states are predicted¹³ to be spread over $\sim 1{GHz}$ (0.03 ${cm}^{-1}$) and the $0_{u}^{+}$ only over $\sim 0.3{GHz}$ (0.01 ${cm}^{-1}$). Furthermore, red of 16 900 ${cm}^{-1}$ the rotational structure is clearly resolved and only the $J = 1, 2, 3$, and $4$ levels appear (see Fig. 4). The absence of the $J = 0$ peak supports the assignment as the component of the $^{1}\Pi_{g}$, which correlate with $1_{g}$ at long range. Finally, Barrow et al.¹⁴ and Effantin et al.¹⁵ have reported vibrational positions for the $^{1}\Pi_{g}$ state below 16 900 ${cm}^{-1}$, which agree with our measurements over the range $v = 38 - 52$.

The long vibrational series shows the transition from $1_{g}$ [Hund's case (c)] character at very long range to $^{1}\Pi_{g}$ [Hund's case (a)] character at closer range. At the low energy (close range) end of our spectrum, the vibrational lines split into rotational peaks, each of which shows a small amount of incompletely resolved hyperfine structure (see Fig. 4). In this regime, the state has very little triplet character, therefore, the hyperfine splittings are small and the transition from the ungerade ground state ($^{3}\Sigma_{u}^{+}$) is forbidden in first order, making the linewidth for this transition less than 1 MHz. At higher energies (longer range) the rotational constant decreases while the state gains more triplet character so the hyperfine structure becomes dominant and the linewidth approaches $\sim 12{MHz}$.¹⁶ Rotational assignment is no longer possible (Fig. 3).

The inset in Fig. 4 illustrates the resolution of our technique. The tail on the red side of the peak comes from the kinetic energy of the free atoms which can add to the photon energy to bring a red photon into resonance. This tail is approximately 15 MHz wide, corresponding to a temperature of $\sim 0.65{mK}$.¹⁷ Theoretically, the sharp onset on the blue side of the $J = 2$ peak is dominated by the s-wave Wigner threshold law.¹⁸ In Fig. 4, this sharpness is limited by the instrumental resolution, of $\sim 2{MHz}$. The $J = 3$ and $4$ peaks show the more gradual p- and d-wave onsets.

Over the full range in which the rotational structure is resolved ($16\,967 - 16\,800{cm}^{-1}$, $v = 38 - 76$) the measured rotational constants agree extremely well with adiabatic potential calculations by Williams and Julienne¹³ (see Fig. 5). In all of the vibrational peaks that show rotational structure, the $J = 1, 2, 3$, and $4$ peaks are prominent. The $J = 5$ peak, which is completely absent, would be due to f-waves ($l = 3$) and higher partial waves. Therefore, we conclude from the data that the Franck-Condon factors for the f-waves and higher partial waves are much smaller than those for the lower partial waves, due to suppression by the centrifugal barrier. The relative contributions of the various partial waves are not easily determined theoretically because small uncertainties in the ground state potentials, especially near dissociation, cause large uncertainties in their Franck-Condon factors.¹³ Experimental information about the magnitude and the positions of the minima of the Franck-Condon factors may aid in determining the ground state scattering length, a parameter important in quantum collective effects in ultracold gases.¹⁹

We have also observed a rovibrational spectrum of the $0_{g}^{-}$ potential that is asymptotically $3^{2}S_{1/2} + 3^{2}P_{3/2}$. The $0_{g}^{-}$ potential has a shallow ($\sim 2{cm}^{-1}$) well due to an avoided crossing with a repulsive $0_{g}^{-}$ potential that is asymptotically $3^{2}S_{1/2} + 3^{2}P_{1/2}$. This well supports only "purely long-range" states⁶,⁷ whose inner turning points ($\sim 55\ a_{0}$ in this case) are beyond the outer turning point of most chemically bound states. These purely long-range states have been previously observed in their effect on the far wings of absorption lines in alkali vapors.²⁰ Recently, structure, which is most likely due to potentials which have some $0_{g}^{-}$ character,²¹ has been observed in $Na_{2}$ within 0.06 ${cm}^{-1}$ of dissociation. The more deeply bound, and shorter range, $0_{g}^{-}$ states of $Rb_{2}$ have also recently been observed in photoassociation.²²

We expect no direct ionization from the purely long-range states because the photon does not have enough energy to ionize the molecule at such a large internuclear separation. Trap loss is possible, however, as the atom pair can escape from the trap upon decay of the excited molecule into energetic free atoms. We observe a vibrational series in the trap loss spectrum in the range, $16\,972.0 - 16\,973.3{cm}^{-1}$. Of the 11 peaks in our spectrum, we are able to accurately determine both the positions and rotational constants for only seven. Because this series is not seen in the probe-phase signal, these values are obtained by fitting the noisier trap loss data to a sum of five Gaussians with a variable width (we ignore any possible distortions to the line shape¹¹). The uncertainties (see Table I) in the rotational constants reflect the spread in results from fitting procedures having different constraints on the widths and relative positions of the lines. As seen in Table I, the vibrational level spacings and the rotational constants are in reasonable agreement with predictions.¹³,²³

Photoassociation is especially suited to the spectroscopy of long range diatomic molecules. We have obtained assignable spectra of the $0_{g}^{-}$ and $0_{u}^{+}$ states of $Na_{2}$ to within 0.1 ${cm}^{-1}$ of dissociation. Our assignment of the $1_{g}$ state stops 0.15 ${cm}^{-1}$ below dissociation not for lack of signal, but because our spectrum becomes too dense and complicated. Our probe-phase ion signal gives a usable dynamic range of at least four decades, allowing us to study transitions that are forbidden in first order. While in our present method the molecule is formed and photoionized by the same color photon, this technique can be extended to other species by introducing a second, ionizing laser of another color or by using other measures of trap loss, such as fluorescence.²,⁵ Finally, we note that such accurate spectroscopy of long range molecules, combined with improved theory, might yield an improved determination of $C_{3}$, and thus the $P_{3/2}$ atomic lifetime.
