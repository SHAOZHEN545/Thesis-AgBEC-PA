Carl J. Williams  
James Franck Institute, University of Chicago, 5640 Ellis Avenue, Chicago, Illinois 60637

Molecular Physics Division, National Institute of Standards and Technology, Gaithersburg, Maryland 20899

*J. Chem. Phys.* **101**, 2634–2637 (1994). DOI: [10.1063/1.467637](https://doi.org/10.1063/1.467637)

## Abstract

The photoassociation of colliding ultracold trapped atoms permits the observation of high resolution molecular spectra of high lying vibrational levels with low total angular momentum and outer turning points greater than 50 Å. This paper describes how the hyperfine structure of alkali dimer molecules at large internuclear separation can be used to assign the electronic character of the observed photoassociation spectra and how hyperfine structure helps to explain the observed photoassociation spectrum of the attractive electronic states of the $Na_2$ molecule that correlate with $Na(3\,{}^2S)+Na(3\,{}^2P_{3/2})$ atoms.

Thorsheim *et al.*<sup>1</sup> pointed out that a new kind of high resolution molecular spectroscopy would be possible with laser cooled and trapped atoms. In this photoassociation spectroscopy<sup>2,3</sup> the frequency of a probe laser is tuned and the signal detected due to the formation of a molecular product by the photoassociation of two colliding ultracold atoms in their ground ${}^2S$ states. The typical experiment involves laser cooling and trapping atoms in a magneto-optical trap (MOT)<sup>4–8</sup> or far off-resonance optical trap (FORT).<sup>9</sup> This kind of spectroscopy has recently been realized experimentally in the one-color<sup>4–6</sup> and two-color<sup>7</sup> photoassociative ionization spectrum of Na atoms, and in the trap loss spectra of Na,<sup>6</sup> Li,<sup>8</sup> and Rb<sup>9</sup> atoms. Detecting product signal vs probe frequency measures the positions of excited bound rovibrational levels of molecular states that correlate with a ground ${}^2S$ atom and an excited ${}^2P$ atom. These bound levels are intermediate states in the scattering process that leads to the product. In Refs. 4–7, the “product” is the $Na_2^+$ molecular ion, and in the trap loss spectra of Refs. 6, 8, and 9, it is an untrapped species, such as hotter atoms or translationally cold, but vibrationally hot diatomic molecules. High resolution is achieved in such spectra, even though the initial state is a continuum state, because the thermal spread of initial kinetic energy of trapped atoms with $T<1\,\mathrm{mK}$ is smaller or of the same order of magnitude as the natural linewidth of the intermediate molecular vibrational states.<sup>1,2</sup>

We believe that photoassociation spectroscopy will prove to be very practical because: (1) it probes molecular structure right up to the dissociation limit, (2) it can excite states that are normally inaccessible to conventional spectroscopy, (3) the spectra clearly exhibit hyperfine structure, which in conjunction with theory provides an unambiguous signature for assigning the electronic character of the spectra, and (4) the line shapes and intensity patterns of the spectra probe ground state collisions in the quantum Wigner-law limit ($T\to0$),<sup>2,10</sup> and are very sensitive to details of the ground and excited state potentials. The Wigner-law threshold effects on spectral line shapes are briefly mentioned in the accompanying paper<sup>6</sup> and we will give a detailed theoretical description elsewhere.<sup>10</sup> Photoassociation spectroscopy should prove to be versatile since it can readily be extended to other homonuclear species using two or more colors and to heteronuclear species, including mixed isotopes. This paper theoretically examines several important features of photoassociation spectroscopy and the underlying hyperfine structure that are relevant to the $Na_2$ spectrum of Refs. 5 and 6. Many of the arguments also apply to the trap loss spectra of Li<sup>8</sup> and Rb.<sup>9</sup>

We have developed a complete formalism for the collision of two identical atoms in a radiation field.<sup>11</sup> Due to the very small initial kinetic energy ($T<1\,\mathrm{mK}$) for ultracold atomic collisions it is essential to incorporate weak spin-dependent interactions responsible for the resolvable fine and hyperfine structure of the photoassociated molecules and free atoms. For example, the splitting between the $f=1$ and $f=2$ hyperfine components of the $Na({}^2S)$ ground state is $E_{\mathrm{hfs}}/h=1.77\,\mathrm{GHz}$. Setting $E_{\mathrm{hfs}}=k_B T_{\mathrm{hfs}}$ gives $T_{\mathrm{hfs}}=85.0\,\mathrm{mK}$, where $k_B$ is Boltzmann’s constant. Thus, our molecular Hamiltonian consists of terms that represent the usual molecular electronic structure, the center-of-mass rotation $\ell$, and spin-dependent interactions. In addition, we also calculate and incorporate the transition dipole matrix $V_{\mathrm{rad}}$, which couples the manifold of molecular electronic states correlating to ground ${}^2S+{}^2S$ atoms with those correlating to ${}^2S+{}^2P$ atoms. The effects of homonuclear symmetry are built directly into the asymptotic Hund’s case (e) “atomic” basis and the molecular Hund’s case (a) basis sets which are used to define the above Hamiltonian. The Hund’s case (e) basis describes the states of infinitely separated noninteracting atoms colliding with relative angular momentum $\ell$. The Hund’s case (a) basis describes the rotating molecule when the splitting between the Born-Oppenheimer $\Sigma$ and $\Pi$ electronic states is large compared to spin-orbit interactions. Finally, a Hund’s case (c) basis is useful to describe the rotating molecule at internuclear separations where the spin-orbit interactions are large compared to the splitting between Born-Oppenheimer $\Sigma$ and $\Pi$ electronic states. The Hund’s case (a), (c), and (e) basis functions are used only as basis sets for diagonalizing the full Hamiltonian and to provide physical insight into the best “first-order” description of the electronic coupling.

The molecular electronic structure is represented by nonrelativistic Born-Oppenheimer molecular potentials for $Na_2$. For the ${}^1\Sigma_g^+$ and ${}^3\Sigma_u^+$ states that correlate with ${}^2S+{}^2S$ atoms we use the same hybrid Rydberg-Klein-Rees (RKR)/*ab initio* potentials as in Ref. 1; for the eight excited state potentials, we use the very accurate effective core potentials of Magnier *et al.*<sup>12</sup> joined smoothly onto the resonant dipole-dipole $C_3/R^3$ form for larger $R$. The measured oscillator strength for the Na atom<sup>13</sup> gives $C_3=-6.175\,e a_0$ for the nonrelativistic Hund’s case (a) ${}^1\Pi_g$ state ($e=$ electronic charge, $a_0=0.0529177\,\mathrm{nm}$). This single $R$-independent $C_3$ value parametrizes the dipole-dipole interaction for all eight nonrelativistic case (a) Born-Oppenheimer states, since any other $C_3$ is related to it by factors of $\pm1$ or $\pm2$. These adiabatic electronic Born-Oppenheimer potentials are transformed into an asymptotic basis by a standard frame transformation between body-fixed and space-fixed quantization axes.<sup>11</sup> Fine and hyperfine structure are represented diagonally in the asymptotic Hund’s case (e) basis in terms of the measured fine and hyperfine splittings of the Na atom and the standard pure precession approximation is used for the center-of-mass rotation. This procedure gives a rigorous quantum mechanical description of all electronic, spin, and rotational degrees of freedom for the colliding atoms.

In the calculations presented below, we simplify this complete description of colliding atoms in a radiation field in order to illustrate several qualitative features of the photoassociation spectrum. First, we diagonalize the Hamiltonian for the manifold of excited states that correlate to ${}^2S+{}^2P$ atoms for each total angular momentum $F=J+I$ and parity to produce a set of adiabatic molecular potentials ($F=L+S+I+\ell$, where the terms are the respective electron orbital, electron spin, nuclear spin, and relative rotational angular momenta with respective projections on the molecular axis of $\Lambda$, $\Sigma$, $\iota$, and 0). These potentials are shown in Figs. 1 and 2 for an even simpler diagonalization that ignores center-of-mass rotation $\ell$, so that the projection $\phi=\Lambda+\Sigma+\iota$ on the molecular axis is a good quantum number. At any given internuclear separation $R$ a diagonally dominant Hund’s case exists. For sufficiently large $R$ the electronic potentials in that Hund’s case representation can be described by a set of $C_3$ coefficients. However, the $R$-dependent diagonalization of the full Hamiltonian insures that the adiabatic (diagonalized) potentials cannot be characterized by an $R$-independent $C_3$.

Figure 1 shows the adiabatic potentials over a range of $\pm100\,\mathrm{GHz}$ (0 GHz is taken as the uppermost hyperfine component of the $Na(3\,{}^2S)+Na(3\,{}^2P_{3/2})$ asymptote), covering the spectral range of Ref. 5. The curves on this scale are essentially the familiar curves of Movre and Pichler.<sup>14</sup> The inset of Fig. 1 shows that states of different symmetry have very different hyperfine splittings. Figure 2 shows a blowup of the long range structure of the adiabatic curves on a scale of 5 GHz; in the absence of molecular rotation, curves with different $\phi$ can cross, but curves of the same $\phi$ undergo avoided crossings. The large number of hyperfine adiabatic states in this region suggests that photoassociation spectra for laser detunings a few GHz red of the atomic transition will be complex (see Refs. 4 and 7), with overlapping features due to many different states. The spectra of Refs. 5, 6, 8, and 9 show well-resolved bound state features, because the laser is detuned far enough to the red of the atomic transition that the spacing between adjacent vibrational levels is large compared to atomic hyperfine structure. We now turn our attention to analyzing the spectra of Refs. 5 and 6.

Hyperfine components of the short range $1_g$ state all correlate adiabatically with the $P(3/2)+S(f=1)$ asymptote. Complex spectra, which involve many overlapping features, have been observed in this range of detuning in Refs. 4, 5, and 7.

Of the five attractive states correlating to ${}^2S+{}^2P_{3/2}$, four are optically active ($1_u$, $0_g^-$, $1_g$, and $0_u^+$), while one ($2_u$) is dipole forbidden. Our calculations of the hyperfine structure of the five attractive states was used to help confirm the assignment of the spectra observed in Ref. 5 to the $1_g$ state. The spectra observed in Ref. 5 span 100 GHz, a range larger than the binding energies of the purely long range $1_u$ and $0_g^-$ states.<sup>14,15</sup> Although the calculated asymptotic $C_3$ coefficient of the $0_u^+$ state is very close to that of the $1_g$ state, our calculated hyperfine splitting of the $0_u^+$ levels is approximately $1/3$ of that of the $1_g$ state (see Fig. 1 inset) for detunings less than 100 GHz and is inconsistent with the breadth of the features observed in Ref. 5. If our calculated numerical eigenvalues obtained from the fully adiabatic electronic rotational hyperfine molecular potentials are fit using the procedure in Ref. 5 to extract an effective $C_3$ coefficient, the fitted value agrees within experimental error with the measured value,<sup>5</sup> $10.37\pm0.07\,e^2a_0^2$ (quoted error is one standard deviation), as long as the measured and calculated levels span the same range. This good agreement demonstrates that our procedure, based on a single $R$-independent $C_3$ coefficient for the Hund’s case (a) nonrelativistic Born-Oppenheimer potentials described above, accurately reproduces the observed level spacings when the full Hamiltonian is diagonalized. However, the effective $C_3$ coefficient is 8% higher than the Hund’s case (c) $C_3$ coefficient for the $1_g$ state without hyperfine structure, which is $9.56\,e a_0$. The reason for this difference is the $R$-dependent diagonalization of the full Hamiltonian results in an $R$-dependent effective $C_3$ coefficient for the $1_g$ state. In other words, the fitted value of $C_3$ will depend on the range of levels fit. It should be noted that the hyperfine components of the $1_g$ state adiabatically correlate with ${}^2P_{3/2}+{}^2S(f=1)$, not ${}^2P_{3/2}+{}^2S(f=2)$ (see Fig. 2), whereas the numerical fitting of the experimental eigenvalue positions extrapolate to a limit much closer to the latter, namely, $0.21\pm0.03\,\mathrm{GHz}$ below ${}^2P_{3/2}(f=3)+{}^2S(f=2)$.<sup>5</sup> In any case the theoretical analysis based on our calculated hyperfine structure permits an unambiguous assignment of the spectrum in Ref. 5 to vibrational levels of the $1_g$ state. The measurement of the $1_g$ spectra to significantly larger detunings in the accompanying paper<sup>6</sup> provides further conclusive proof of that assignment.

Figure 3 presents calculated spectra for ${}^2S(f=1)+{}^2S(f=1)$ photoassociation to various vibrational levels of the $1_g$ state, which is a mixture of ${}^3\Sigma_g$, ${}^3\Pi_g$, and ${}^1\Pi_g$ case (a) states at long range. These come from calculating Franck-Condon factors using spherically averaged transition dipoles to each individual excited adiabatic electronic rotational hyperfine molecular level. The approximate Lorentzian line shapes assumed here are adequate at low resolution, but we calculate elsewhere<sup>10</sup> the actual asymmetric line shapes<sup>1</sup> needed for high resolution studies. Both the oscillator strength and the hyperfine structure of the $1_g$ state is due to its triplet character. Since the $1_g$ state at short range loses triplet character and adiabatically correlates with the ${}^1\Pi_g$ state, the $1_g$ spectrum becomes much weaker and progressively exhibits less hyperfine structure as the laser detunes to the red and excites more deeply bound levels. This is evident from Fig. 3, which shows calculated spectra for $v=59$, 67, 74, and 80. The $v=59$ level shows four isolated clumps corresponding to the $J=1$, 2, 3, and 4 rotational levels ($J=4$ is off scale in Fig. 3). Only ground state $s$, $p$, and $d$ waves ($\ell=0$, 1, and 2, respectively) contribute to the photoassociation spectrum at the MOT temperature of about $650\,\mu\mathrm{K}$, and the intensity of the $J=4$ level is due entirely to the $d$-wave. The spectrum does not have contributions beyond $d$-waves because the initial kinetic energy of the atoms insures that the amplitude of the ground state wave function is negligible at short internuclear distances because of the existence of the centrifugal barrier. This causes the resulting Franck-Condon factor to become negligible. However, it should be noted that the $d$-wave barrier is already higher than the initial kinetic energy and that penetration of the ground state wave function to short distances is extremely sensitive to the existence of a bound or resonant state very near threshold in the ground potential.<sup>10</sup> For larger red detunings (e.g., see $v=48$ in accompanying paper<sup>6</sup>) no hyperfine structure appears on an energy scale of a few GHz; however, residual hyperfine structure appears when such data is further resolved. As $v$ increases from $v=59$ toward dissociation the hyperfine structure that results from the increasing triplet character of the $1_g$ state becomes larger and the rotational constant $B_v$ becomes smaller. Thus, at sufficiently high $v$, e.g., $v=80$ in Fig. 3, $1_g$ rotational structure merges into a single hyperfine “clump” and $J$ is no longer a “good” quantum number, leaving only $F=J+I$ as a good quantum number. The nonadiabaticity within the hyperfine manifold of $1_g$ states remains to be fully understood, and our present analysis is adiabatic only. As shown in the accompanying paper,<sup>6</sup> our calculated rotational constants $1_g$ levels where $J$ is approximately good are in excellent agreement with the measured ones. These calculated constants are fully *ab initio*, giving a zero parameter fit to the data.

The accompanying paper<sup>6</sup> also reports observations of vibrational levels of $0_g^-$ and $0_u^+$ states correlating with $Na({}^2P_{3/2})+Na({}^2S)$ atoms. The observed hyperfine splittings of the $0_u^+$ features are much less than for the $1_g$ features and are consistent with our calculations. The $0_g^-$ state is a special pure long range molecular state<sup>15</sup> with a very small depth and inner turning points larger than $50\,a_0$ (see Fig. 1). We predict that there will be negligible hyperfine splitting at the low vibrational levels of the $0_g^-$, although higher vibrational levels have widths due to unresolved hyperfine structure. These predictions are consistent with the observed spectra in Ref. 6, which gives a comparison of predicted and measured vibrational bands.

Finally, we note the prospects of new classes of experiments based on various detection schemes for the photoassociation process. Translationally cold bound ground state molecules can be made as one of the products<sup>1,2</sup> and are probably one of the products produced in trap loss spectra.<sup>6,8,9</sup> It may be possible to trap such molecules, which could be confined in a suitable far-off-resonance optical trap,<sup>9</sup> or to detect them by multiphoton ionization. Also, using an extra color of light to control the final state product to which the intermediate level decays offers very rich prospects for new spectroscopic probes of long range molecular states or of the photoionization of other long range intermediate resonances. For example, the one-color photoionization of the $1_g$ state in $Na_2$ is possible due to very favorable Franck-Condon overlap of its vibrational wave function near its inner turning point with vibrational wave functions of the molecular ion. Using a second color to obtain favorable Franck-Condon factors for other intermediate states of $Na_2$ will allow additional states to be observed in photoionization as well. Using two colors, the ionization of intermediate resonances should be possible for the other alkali species, since two photons of a single color to the red of the cooling transition do not have enough energy to produce molecular ions, except for Na. Tuning the frequency of the second photon could give the spectrum of the ionization process. Experiments of this type have already been done in limited regions of very small detuning.<sup>7</sup>
