# Photoassociative spectroscopy of ultracold $^{39}\mathrm{K}$ atoms in a high-density vapor-cell magneto-optical trap

H. Wang, P. L. Gould, and W. C. Stwalley  
Department of Physics, U-46, University of Connecticut, Storrs, Connecticut 06269  
(Received 3 August 1995)

## Abstract

We have performed photoassociative spectroscopy of ultracold potassium atoms in a high-density vapor-cell magneto-optical trap (MOT) in which atoms are confined predominantly in "dark" hyperfine levels that do not interact with the trapping light. Three well-resolved vibrational series (one strong and two weak) of the $^{39}\mathrm{K}_2$ molecule are observed within $8\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ below the $4^2P_{3/2}$ atomic limit. One of the series is identified as the $0_g^-$ pure long-range state and the other two are assigned to the $0_u^+$ and $1_g$ states. These photoassociative spectra are obtained by measuring reductions in the trap fluorescence due to trap loss induced by an intense photoassociation laser. By using a vapor-cell-dark MOT technique, we achieve atomic densities greater than $10^{11}\ \mathrm{cm}^{-3}$, increasing our trap loss signals by an order of magnitude compared to a normal MOT.

PACS number(s): 33.80.Ps, 33.20.-t, 34.20.-b

The long-range region of the potential-energy curves between interacting atoms has long interested both physicists and chemists [1-6] because of its importance in the understanding of interatomic forces, weakly bound highly excited molecules, ultracold collisions, and formation of ultracold molecules. Determination of long-range potentials based on conventional molecular spectroscopy, however, is difficult because normally the Franck-Condon factors are extremely small for electronic transitions from the thermally populated region of the bound ground-state to vibrational levels near dissociation. Recently, all-optical multiple resonance spectroscopy has been used in our group to study many highly excited singlet and triplet electronic states of alkali-metal-atom dimers out to internuclear distances $R \sim 20\ \mathrm{\AA}$ [7-9]. These experimentally determined potential-energy curves have provided excellent tests for both _ab initio_ calculations based on molecular structure and the long-range perturbation theory calculations of the atom-atom interaction. For example, a modified distance, $R_{\mathrm{LR}-m}$, has been proposed to represent a practical criterion for a long-range radius where perturbation theory is valid by comparing the long-range portion of the potential curves with _ab initio_ and long-range calculations [10]. However, many interesting aspects of long-range interactions, such as "pure long-range" molecular states [5], nonadiabatic fine and hyperfine inelastic exchange collisions [11], and retardation effects [2] occur at even longer internuclear distances (a few tens to a few hundreds of angstroms), where even the powerful all-optical multiple resonance spectroscopy becomes impractical.

Photoassociative spectroscopy of ultracold atoms, originally proposed by Thorsheim _et al._ [12], has proven to be an extremely powerful technique to investigate such long-range molecular states and interatomic potentials [13-20] as a logical complement to traditional "short-range" molecular spectroscopy. The techniques of laser cooling and trapping have produced ultracold atomic gases ($T < 1\ \mathrm{mK}$) with very narrow kinetic energy ($<20\ \mathrm{MHz}$) and angular-momentum (e.g., $s$, $p$, and $d$ waves for $^{39}\mathrm{K}$) distributions. This provides a new opportunity for long-range molecular spectroscopy by means of free-bound transitions from the free state of two interacting atoms with resolution comparable to bound-bound excitations. High-resolution photoassociative spectra of long-range molecular states of alkali-metal diatomics have recently been reported for $\mathrm{Na}_2$ [13,14], $\mathrm{Rb}_2$ [15-17], and $\mathrm{Li}_2$ [18-20]. It is worth noting that this new type of spectroscopy is also useful in the precise measurement of atomic radiative lifetimes by spectroscopically determining the long-range interaction constant $C_3$ [18] and in determinations of the $s$-wave scattering length [17,19,20], which is an important parameter for the realization of Bose-Einstein condensation [21,22]. The intensity pattern and line shape of photoassociation spectra can provide detailed information on both ground- and excited-state wave functions [23]. Such photoassociation techniques also offer a future opportunity to prepare ultracold molecules [24].

The case of potassium is unique among the alkali-metal atoms because the hyperfine levels of the excited state are noninverted and closely spaced (all within 34 MHz of each other), resulting in strong optical pumping (i.e., no effective cycling transition) when typical laser detunings are used. It is only recently that potassium atoms were trapped in a vapor-cell magneto-optical trap (MOT) by Williamson and Walker [25]. As a direct consequence of the small hyperfine splittings, detuning below all excited-state hyperfine components and strong optical repumping was found to optimize the capture and confinement of potassium atoms.

The rate equation for the total number $N$ of trapped atoms in a vapor-cell trap can be described as [26]

$$
\frac{dN}{dt}=L-\left[(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)+(\beta_c+\beta_{\mathrm{PA}})n_c\right]N, \tag{1}
$$

where $n_c$ is the constant trap density, $L$ the trap loading rate, and $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ are trap-loss rates due to background gas collisions and untrapped potassium atom collisions, respectively. $\beta_c$ and $\beta_{\mathrm{PA}}$ represent the cold collisional trap-loss coefficients induced by the trap laser and the photoassociation laser, respectively.

In the steady state, $dN/dt=0$, and thus Eq. (1) yields

$$
N=\frac{L}{(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)+(\beta_c+\beta_{\mathrm{PA}})n_c}. \tag{2}
$$

As can be seen from Eq. (2), in order to observe a trap-loss spectrum induced by photoassociation, the term $\beta_{\mathrm{PA}} n_c$ in Eq. (2) must compete with the sum of all other trap losses, that is, $(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)+\beta_c n_c$. Since the photoassociation rate is proportional to the atomic density in the trap, higher trap density and lower background loss rate (longer trap lifetime) are preferred conditions for photoassociative spectroscopy. For example, a far-off-resonance dipole force trap (FORT) is used in the Rb photoassociation experiments to increase the trap density to $\sim 10^{12}\ \mathrm{atoms}/\mathrm{cm}^3$ [15-17]. In the Li [18,19] and Na [13,14] experiments, extremely low background pressure and MOTs loaded from atomic beams are used to reduce the background gas collision trap loss. A vapor-cell MOT is much simpler and less expensive for generating ultracold atoms [27,28]. However, the trap loss due to collisions with untrapped hot atoms in the vapor cell [the term $\gamma_2$ in Eq. (2)] is not negligible, making it more challenging to perform photoassociative spectroscopy in a simple vapor-cell MOT.

The idea of a "dark-spot" trap, first developed by Ketterle _et al._ at MIT [29], is to reduce the fraction of excited-state population by applying weak or no repumping light to the center of the trap. Higher atomic density, e.g., $10^{12}\ \mathrm{atoms}/\mathrm{cm}^3$ in Ref. [29], can be achieved due to the reduction of (i) excited-state collision-induced trap loss, and (ii) the repulsive interatomic force due to radiation trapping. It is obvious from Eq. (2) that this dark-spot trap technique will greatly benefit measurements of the photoassociation-induced trap loss $\beta_{\mathrm{PA}}$ by increasing the trap density and simultaneously reducing the cold collision trap loss $\beta_c$. It has been noticed that since such a dark-spot trap relies on effectively pumping the trapped atoms into the dark hyperfine level, it works better for atoms with smaller hyperfine splittings (strong optical pumping) such as Na and K than those with larger splittings like $^{87}\mathrm{Rb}$ for which a separate laser [resonant with the $5^2S_{1/2}(F=2) \to 5^2P_{3/2}(F'=2)$ transition] is needed to force the atoms into the dark $F=1$ state (forced dark-spot trap) [30]. Indeed, when the "dark-spot" trap technique is used in the Na photoassociation experiment with an atomic-beam-loaded MOT, an increase in the photoassociative ionization rate by 100-fold is obtained [14]. In our potassium experiment described below, we have successfully applied the dark-spot trap idea to a potassium vapor-cell MOT and achieved an increase in the trap-loss signals by more than one order of magnitude. In this paper we first describe the dark-spot vapor-cell potassium MOT, which allowed us to perform the photoassociative spectroscopy. Then we present the results of the photoassociative spectroscopy of ultracold $^{39}\mathrm{K}$.

Initially we built a normal room-temperature vapor-cell magneto-optical trap [27,28] in a ten-arm stainless steel ultra-high-vacuum chamber with a background pressure of $5 \times 10^{-10}\ \mathrm{Torr}$. The trapping laser beams are provided by a Coherent 899-29 single-mode tunable ring dye laser with output power of 300 mW at 766.5 nm. The ring dye laser frequency is locked to the $^{39}\mathrm{K}\ 4^2S_{1/2}(F=2) \to 4^2P_{3/2}(F'=3)$ transition using saturated absorption. A 40-MHz red detuning below the upper $F'=3$ level is generated by an acousto-optic modulator (AOM). The optical repumping beam with a 462-MHz frequency shift is obtained by double passing through a 231-MHz AOM. The total intensities at the trapping region are $90\ \mathrm{mW}/\mathrm{cm}^2$ for the trapping beams and $40\ \mathrm{mW}/\mathrm{cm}^2$ for the repumping beams. At room temperature this cell MOT captures $\sim 2 \times 10^7$ atoms with a trap density of $\sim 3 \times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{atoms}/\mathrm{cm}^3$. We observe a trap lifetime of $\sim 3\ \mathrm{s}$. The temperature of the trapped atoms is estimated by a ballistic technique to be $500\ \mu\mathrm{K}$. In order to achieve higher trap density we modified the above vapor-cell MOT into a "dark-spot" MOT. The center region of the optical repumping beam is blocked by an opaque disk of 2 mm in diameter before being combined with the horizontal trapping beam. There is no repumper in the vertical beam. The trap density of the dark-spot trap is estimated to be greater than $10^{11}\ \mathrm{atoms}/\mathrm{cm}^3$ and the photoassociation-induced trap-loss rate is increased by one order of magnitude. We believe that the increase in the photoassociation rate is mainly due to the enhancement of the trap density, because no significant change in trap lifetime (or total trap loss) is observed when the dark spot is inserted.

A separate intense autoscan cw single-mode ring Ti:sapphire laser (Coherent 899-29) with output power of about 1-2 W is used to induce the photoassociation of ultracold potassium atoms. This intense laser beam is focused to a diameter of about 0.5 mm at the trap region. The laser frequency is calibrated by uranium atomic lines. When the laser frequency is resonant with a free-bound transition, $\mathrm{K}_2$ molecules are formed by the photoassociation process. These excited long-range molecules predominantly decay either into ground-state molecules or into free atoms with enough kinetic energy to escape from the trap. Since both processes lead to a loss of atoms from the trap, a photoassociative resonance will result in a decrease in trap fluorescence intensity that is monitored by a photomultiplier tube.

Figure 1 presents the photoassociative spectrum of $^{39}\mathrm{K}_2$ long-range vibrational states in a region spanning about $8\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ below the $^{39}\mathrm{K}\ 4^2P_{3/2}$ limit. First we note that decreases of $\sim 20\%$ in trap fluorescence intensity at resonances have been observed with the "dark-spot" vapor-cell MOT. This represents an increase of more than one order of magnitude in the photoassociation rate (relative to other loss rates) compared to a normal vapor-cell MOT where these dips in trap fluorescence intensity were comparable to the background noise.

Three well-resolved vibrational series (one strong and two weak) dissociating to the $4^2P_{3/2}$ limit are identified from the spectrum in Fig. 1. Among the ten Hund's case-$c$ components dissociating to the $\mathrm{K}\ 4^2P_{3/2}$ limit, two attractive states, $0_u^+$ and $1_g$, and two "pure long-range" states [5], $0_g^-$ and $1_u$, are optically accessible from the three ground-state case-$c$ components, $0_g^+$, $0_u^-$, and $1_u$, via free-bound transitions. The $0_u^+$ and $1_g$ states of the $^2P_{3/2}$ limit correlate adiabatically at short internuclear distance to Hund's case-$a$ states, $b^3\Pi_{u,\Omega=0}$ and $1^1\Pi_g$, respectively. The two "pure long-range" states ($0_g^-$ and $1_u$) of $^{39}\mathrm{K}_2$, formed by avoided crossings of long-range case-$c$ potentials, are calculated to have very shallow potential wells of $6.48\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ at $R_e=28.7\ \mathrm{\AA}$ and $0.54\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ at $R_e=39.7\ \mathrm{\AA}$, respectively [31].

We assign one of the weak vibrational series to the $0_g^-$ "pure long-range" state based on the eigenvalues calculated by Bussery and Aubert-Frecon [31]. The lowest 12 observed vibrational levels are fitted, giving the molecular constants listed in Table I. Also listed in Table I are the calculated constants for comparison. Further improvements to the "dark-spot" trap system should enable us to resolve the rotational structure and construct the experimentally determined potential-energy curve for this state.

For the other two vibrational series, we assign the strong one to the $0_u^+$ state and the weak one to the $1_g$ state based on our measured values of the long-range dipole-dipole interaction constant $C_3$. It is well known that at large internuclear distance the resonance dipole interatomic potential between $\mathrm{K}(4s)$ and $\mathrm{K}(4p)$ atoms is described by $V(R)=D-C_3/R^3$, where $D$ is the dissociation energy and $R$ the internuclear distance. The long-range vibrational binding energies can then be described as $D-E_v=[a/(\mu^3 C_3^2)](v_D-v)^6$ [3,4], where $E_v$ is the energy of the vibrational level $v$ (measured from the minimum of the potential well), $a$ is a known constant, $\mu$ is the reduced mass of $^{39}\mathrm{K}_2$, and $v_D$ is the effective (noninteger) vibrational quantum number at the dissociation limit. If the long-range potential is purely characterized by an $R^{-3}$ dependence, the one-sixth power of the binding energies, $(D-E_v)^{1/6}$, should depend linearly on the vibrational quantum number $v$ with the slope yielding the long-range molecular constant $C_3$. Note that the 24 vibrational levels of the strong series and the 15 levels of the weak series that we have observed have outer turning points at internuclear distances $R=74-142$ bohr and $74-113$ bohr, respectively. In the case of potassium, the fine-structure splitting for the $4s+4p$ limit is $57.7\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$; an $R^{-3}$ potential of this magnitude corresponds to an internuclear distance $R_{\mathrm{FS}}\sim 32$ bohr. As $R$ increases toward $R_{\mathrm{FS}}$, the potentials evolve from Hund's case $a$ to case $c$. For regions where $D-E_v \ll \Delta E_{\mathrm{FS}}$, the relations (in atomic units) $C_3(0_u^+)=(5/3)d^2$ and $C_3(1g)=(\sqrt{7}+2)d^2/3$ hold for the two observed case-$c$ states where $d=\langle 4s|\mu|4p\rangle$ is the resonance dipole matrix element of the $4s+4p$ atomic system [11]. On the other hand, since the hyperfine splitting of the ground state of $^{39}\mathrm{K}$ is only 462 MHz ($0.015\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$), the hyperfine effects on the case-$c$ potentials in the region of $1-8\ \mathrm{cm}^{-1}$ below the atomic limit are still small. Indeed, the good linearities of such plots [$(D-E_v)^{1/6}$ vs $v$] indicate that the $R^{-3}$ character dominates at these large internuclear distances for the two observed Hund case-$c$ long-range states. A linear least-squares fit of the data gives the long-range molecular constants: $C_3(0_u^+)=14.14 \pm 0.05\ \mathrm{a.u.}$ and $C_3(1g)=13.54 \pm 0.10\ \mathrm{a.u.}$ These compare favorably with the theoretical values of 14.44 and 13.41 a.u., respectively, obtained using $d^2=8.665\ \mathrm{a.u.}$, as calculated by Marinescu and Dalgarno [32], and the relations $C_3(0_u^+)=(5/3)d^2$ and $C_3(1g)=(\sqrt{7}+2)d^2/3$ [11]. The uncertainties for the experimental values represent the statistical uncertainties for those two particular fits. Vibrational progressions with resolved substructures below the $4^2S_{1/2}+4^2P_{1/2}$ limit have recently been observed and will be reported separately.

The typical linewidth of the strong vibrational series ($0_u^+$ state) in the spectrum is about 1-2 GHz, and no substructure (rotational or hyperfine) has been resolved under the present experimental conditions. Theoretical calculations by Williams and Julienne [23] on the molecular hyperfine structure of $\mathrm{Na}_2$ predict that the hyperfine structure spread of a $0_u^+$ level is $\sim 0.3\ \mathrm{GHz}$, approximately 1/3 that of a $1_g$ level. A similar situation occurs for the $\mathrm{K}_2$ molecules [33]. For the ultracold potassium atoms with kinetic energy $\sim 500\ \mu\mathrm{K}$, only rotational levels with $J=0$ and 1 make significant contributions to the photoassociation spectrum, with levels $J>1$ effectively suppressed by the centrifugal barriers. However, the vibrational levels of this $0_u^+$ state can also be broadened by predissociation at a short internuclear distance due to spin-orbit mixing with the $A^1\Sigma_u^+$ state which dissociates to the lower $4^2S_{1/2}+4^2P_{1/2}$ limit [11]. Line broadening due to such predissociation of the $0_u^+$ state of $^{85}\mathrm{Rb}$ has been reported [16]. We therefore tentatively attribute the broad linewidth of the observed vibrational levels of the $0_u^+$ state primarily to a combination of power broadening by the intense photoassociation laser and the predissociation of this state.

In conclusion, we have achieved high densities ($>10^{11}\ \mathrm{cm}^{-3}$) of cold potassium atoms in a "dark-spot" vapor-cell MOT. This simple and inexpensive dark-spot cell MOT is a convenient apparatus for investigation of photoassociative spectroscopy. Hund's case-$c$ long-range molecular states of $0_u^+$ and $1_g$ symmetry and the "pure long-range" state of $0_g^-$ of $^{39}\mathrm{K}_2$ have been observed and studied.
