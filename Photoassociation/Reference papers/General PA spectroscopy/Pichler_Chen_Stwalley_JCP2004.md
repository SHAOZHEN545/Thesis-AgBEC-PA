## Abstract

High precision photoassociation spectroscopy is performed in ultracold cesium gas, with detunings as large as 51 cm$^{-1}$ below the Cs($6S_{1/2}$) + Cs($6P_{3/2}$) asymptote. Trap-loss fluorescence detection is used for detecting the photoassociation to excited state ultracold molecules. Long vibrational progressions are assigned to electronic states of $0_g^-$, $0_u^+$, and $1_g$ symmetry. The spectral data are fitted to a LeRoy-Bernstein equation, in order to obtain the effective coefficients of the leading long-range interaction term ($C_3/R^3$) and the relative vibrational quantum numbers measured down from dissociation. Additionally we present evidence for perturbations between the $0_g^-$ state and the dark $2_u$ state.

## I. Introduction

Studies of long-range interactions between atoms have long been a topic of interest for both physicists and chemists,[1â€“9] because of their role in understanding weakly bound excited state molecules, ultracold collisions, and formation of ultracold molecules. The recent development of techniques for laser cooling and trapping of atoms has enabled photoassociative spectroscopy of the alkali metals,[10â€“14] and the alkaline earth metals[15] as well as helium[16] and hydrogen.[17] These results have much improved the long-range part of molecular potential curves, as well as binding and dissociation energies. In addition, it is possible to obtain information on the photoassociating atoms such as atomic lifetimes and ground state scattering lengths crucial for the prediction of the stability of Bose-Einstein condensates (BEC).[18â€“20] Photoassociation (PA) as a new spectroscopic technique has been reviewed by Stwalley and Wang,[21] whereas Weiner and co-workers have reviewed the more general topic of ultracold collisions.[22] Recently, ultracold atoms have been photoassociated by one-[23,24] and two-photon processes in quantum degenerate gases.[25,26] Interestingly, photoassociation of quantum degenerate boson or fermions may lead to a BEC of molecules.

Ultracold cesium PA has been studied by Pillet and co-workers in Orsay, leading to the first observation of translationally ultracold molecules below the two ground state atoms asymptote.[11,27] Spectroscopic studies of excited states dissociating to $6S_{1/2} + 6P_{3/2}$ asymptote[28,29] were carried out by the same group. With the intention to further extend to larger detuning and complement those results, in this paper we present the complete set of observed long and pure long-range states of the Cs dimer below the $6S_{1/2} + 6P_{3/2}$ asymptote detected by trap loss. Our analysis is based on the semiclassical LeRoy-Bernstein approach.[30,31] In our recent paper,[32] we have presented similar work on PA spectroscopy of ultracold cesium below the $6S_{1/2} + 6P_{1/2}$ asymptote.

## II. Experimental Setup

The experiment was performed in a vapor cell magneto-optical trap (MOT). Briefly, this trap consists of three pairs of mutually orthogonal laser beams with opposite $\sigma^{+/-}$ polarizations, and a quadrupole inhomogenous magnetic field produced by two coils with opposite currents. The trapping laser beams were ${\sim}10$ MHz red detuned from the $6S_{1/2}$, $F = 4 \to 6P_{3/2}$, $F = 5$ cycling transition. Additional, repumping laser beams pumped the atoms out of the untrapped $6S_{1/2}$, $F = 3$ ground hyperfine state. We used external cavity diode lasers for trapping and repumping beams, locked and stabilized with a Cs saturated absorption setup. Since no "dark-spot" device was used, almost all the atoms were in the $F = 4$ hyperfine level of the $6S_{1/2}$ ground state, allowing only $f = 4 + f = 4$ binary collisions to occur. During the entire experimental run, the MOT chamber, evacuated to $5 \times 10^{-10}$ Torr was loaded from a cesium getter source. This enabled controlled loading by setting and adjusting the current through the getter.

The PA experiment is shown schematically in Fig. 1. Photoassociation was performed by continuously illuminating Cs atoms with a PA laser. A Ti:sapphire ring laser (Coherent 899-29) pumped by an argon ion laser was used for this purpose. The PA-laser spectral linewidth was $\leqslant 1$ MHz, and its frequency could be scanned continuously in 10 GHz segments. Typical laser power was 450â€“900 mW. The PA-laser beam was focused down to a few hundred micrometer diameter in the cold cloud region. The PA-laser intensity was gradually reduced by neutral density filters for the scans closer to the atomic lines. The frequency of the PA laser was scanned over a wide region below the $6P_{3/2}$ atomic asymptote (up to 51 cm$^{-1}$ to the red of the atomic lines). The Cs $6S_{1/2}$, $F \to 6P_J$, $F'$ transition peaks were used as absolute frequency markers. The continuity and linearity of the frequency scans were monitored from the scanning Vernier etalon signal of the laser wavelength meter. The precision of the laser wave number was $\pm 0.007$ cm$^{-1}$. Typical lengths of the scans were from 2.3 to 4 cm$^{-1}$. The relative laser frequency was frequently checked against the atomic transition. In the case of a laser mode hop, or a bad scan segment, the entire scan was repeated.

So-called trap-loss detection was used for the detection of the photoassociation to excited state molecules. In essence one observes the atomic fluorescence on the $6P_{3/2} \to 6S_{1/2}$ transition. Monitoring this fluorescence versus the probe laser frequency provides the trap-loss spectrum. The mechanism for this detection can be explained as follows: the two photoassociated atoms form a molecule in a given rovibrational level in an excited attractive molecular state, and therefore start to accelerate towards each other, while gaining kinetic energy. The excited molecule decays either into two ground state atoms or into a molecule in an electronic state correlating with the ground state asymptote. Since the center of mass motion is conserved, the atoms gain velocity, and if this velocity exceeds the capture velocity, $v_c$, the atoms escape from the trap. The formed molecule on the other hand is not trapped in a MOT. In either the accelerated atom or the molecule case there is a loss of atoms from the trap, causing a decrease in the atomic fluorescence on the trapping transition. This fluorescence is monitored through a narrowband interference filter with a photomultiplier. The disadvantage of this detection is that pairs of atoms which do not have enough local kinetic energy in the excited state (e.g., in the low levels of $0_g^-$ or $1_u$ pure long-range molecule states[2,21]) do not leave the trap and are not detected. Molecular resonances for detunings greater than 51 cm$^{-1}$ below the Cs D2 line in our experiment were not detected via trap-loss detection because of the decreased photoassociation rate compared to levels with smaller detuning.

## III. Spectral Results and Discussion

The photoassociative process for cesium considered here is

$$
2\text{Cs}(6S_{1/2}, F=4) + h(\nu_0 - \Delta_L) \to \text{Cs}_2[\Omega_{g,u}, v, J, (6P_{3/2})], \tag{1}
$$

where $\nu_0$ is the frequency of the $6S_{1/2} \to 6P_{3/2}$ resonance line and $\Delta_L$ is the red detuning of the laser. Since the two colliding ground state atoms have either $0_g^+$, $0_u^-$, or $1_u$ symmetry, four attractive long-range Hund's case c states can be populated, $0_u^+$, $0_g^-$, $1_g$, and $1_u$. However, the transition to the attractive $2_u$ state is electric dipole forbidden.

The entire trap-loss spectrum is shown in Figs. 2â€“4. The probed photoassociation region extends down to 51 cm$^{-1}$ below the asymptotic limit. Using the calculated widths of spectral features,[28] long vibrational progressions have been assigned to the $0_u^+$ and $1_g$ long-range states and to the $0_g^-$ pure long-range state. The $1_u$ pure long-range state has not been identified in the trap-loss spectrum. Since the potential well of the $1_u$ state is only about 7 cm$^{-1}$ deep, it not only falls in the region of high density of states of other symmetries, causing overlap with other states, but the released kinetic energy is much smaller compared to other states, causing a reduced loss of atoms from the trap. This state also shows strong coupling between the hyperfine interaction and rotation, which is manifested in broad 5â€“6 GHz irregular linewidths.[27]

It is important to notice that almost all observed levels originate from colliding ground state atoms in the $F = 4$ hyperfine state. This is in agreement with the fact that most of the atoms in the trap are in the higher ground hyperfine state, which is due to the presence of repumping light in all six beams and the larger hyperfine splitting in Cs (9.18 GHz), compared to other alkalis. However, several very small features in the region detuned by $\Delta = 20$â€“25 cm$^{-1}$ most likely originate from the $F = 4 + F = 3$ collisions, and are blue detuned compared to other features by the ground state hyperfine structure splitting, $\Delta E_\text{hfs} = 9.18$ GHz. There are, however, no apparent features originating from the $F = 3 + F = 3$ ground state collisions.

We have observed seven additional weak levels (marked with an arrow in Figs. 3 and 4) in the spectral region with detunings of $\Delta = 35$â€“42 cm$^{-1}$. These levels, listed in Table V are possibly a result of rare perturbations between the $0_g^-$ and the $2_u$ state. Such perturbations are forbidden by ordinary $\Delta\Omega = 0, \pm 1$ first-order perturbation selection rules. However, according to Fig. 1, at these detunings, the $0_g^-$ and the $2_u$ potential curves, which nearly overlap at large distance, begin slowly to deviate from one another as $R$ is decreased. This is a consequence of the asymptotic value of the $C_3$ long-range dispersion coefficient being identical for these two states. Under such circumstances, we believe that these additional resonances may be explained by the $2_u$ state borrowing intensity by a second order ($\Delta\Omega = \pm 2$) hyperfine mixing with the $0_g^-$ perturbing state. The observed resonances span a spectral window of less than 10 cm$^{-1}$, after which this second-order perturbation (the involved symmetries differ by 2 u of angular momenta) becomes too small for further intensity borrowing. In the same spectral window, this perturbation affects the levels of $0_g^-$ state as well, by slightly shifting the line positions. Careful examination by the authors of the $0_g^-$ spectra in Ref. 28, showed similar changes in the vibrational level spacing within this spectral window. More quantitative treatment of this rare perturbation effect goes beyond the scope of present paper and will be the presented elsewhere.

The experimental levels were fitted to the semiclassical LeRoy-Bernstein equation,[30,31]

$$
v_D - v = \frac{4 a_n}{h(n-2)} \sqrt{2\mu}\, C_n^{1/n}\, \epsilon_v^{(n-2)/2n} \tag{2}
$$

with

$$
a_n = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} \frac{\Gamma(1/2 + 1/n)}{\Gamma(1 + 1/n)}, \tag{3}
$$

where $v_D$ is the vibrational quantum number at dissociation (typically a noninteger, equal to an integer $[v_D]$ plus a remainder $\delta_D$), $v$ is the vibrational quantum number of a vibrational level, $h$ is Planck's constant, $\mu$ is the Cs$_2$ reduced mass, $C_n$ is the leading long-range coefficient, and $\epsilon_v$ is the binding energy of level $v$. With a linear fit of $(v_D - v)$ versus $\epsilon_v^{(n-2)/2n}$ we have obtained the values for $C_n$, and the $v_D$ for each of observed symmetries.

The obtained $C_3$ values for the $0_u^+$ and $1_g$ states are in good agreement with the experimental values of Comparat et al.[28] In addition our results give an improved precision. Furthermore, there is good agreement for the $0_u^+$ state and ${\sim}9\%$ difference for the $1_g$ state $C_3$ values with the theoretical values of Marinescu and Dalgarno.[33] However, there are differences in $C_3$ value for the $0_g^-$ state. Our value is closest to the experimental value of Fioretti et al.[11] (15% larger). Although in both cases the same formula (2) was used, we believe that the difference arises from the different spectral ranges included in the fits. We have checked our calculation by using the $v = 70$â€“100 levels of Ref. 29 obtained from the Rydberg-Klein-Rees potential. The fit yields $C_3$ of 12.2 a.u., which is well within the error bars of the experimental result of Ref. 11. There is less agreement with combined experimental and theoretical results of Fioretti et al.[29] and Amiot et al.[34] The result of Ref. 29 relies on accurate $B_v$ values. However, the rotational levels for the 57 vibrational levels closest to the dissociation limit were not observed. Thus we are somewhat skeptical of the $C_3$ value in Ref. 29. The difference with the Ref. 34 result is even greater. There is also a notable difference between results of Refs. 29 and 34 evident in Fig. 5 of Ref. 34, which shows a curve which is shifted outwards around the minimum of the potential and inwards at large $R$ compared to the RKR curve. While the RKR potential may be imperfect at long range because of the lack of $B_v$ values, we do not believe it should be inaccurate near the minimum. Thus we believe that Ref. 34 is in error near the minimum. Figure 6 of Ref. 34 shows the differences in rotational constants, which are especially pronounced for levels near the potential minimum and close to the dissociation. This disagreement then translates into an even larger difference in $C_3$ value of present work and that of Ref. 34.

There are approximately 150 additional vibrational levels in each of the three states above the highest clearly observed level ($v_D - v \approx 150$ in Tables Iâ€“III). Such a high density of states is expected for heavier alkali molecules like Cs$_2$ near the dissociation limit. Note, however, that careful consideration of interactions among the long-range states at the two asymptotes ($6S_{1/2} + 6P_{3/2}$ and $6S_{1/2} + 6P_{1/2}$) as well as rotational and hyperfine structure (especially for the $1_g$ state) has not been included, so these $C_n$ values should be considered effective $C_n$ values.

The $1_g$ state is expected to predissociate, due to coupling with the repulsive $0_g^+(6P_{1/2})$ state at long internuclear distances, as in the K$_2$ case.[35] The vibrational features of the $1_g$ state in addition to the mixing of rotations with the hyperfine interactions, will show broadening from predissociation. The $0_u^+$ state likewise is expected to predissociate for more complex reasons, as spelled out for K$_2$.[35,36]

## IV. Conclusion

We have performed high-resolution photoassociative spectroscopy of ultracold cesium atoms. By controlling the cesium background pressure and therefore the trap loading, we were able to achieve high fractional trap loss and observe spectral features down to 51 cm$^{-1}$ below the $6P_{3/2}$ asymptote. The observed vibrational progressions assigned to the $0_u^+$ and $1_g$ long range, and the $0_g^-$ pure long-range states were fitted to the LeRoy-Bernstein equation. The high level of linearity in the fits enabled precise determination of the effective $C_3$ leading long-range coefficients together with the vibrational quantum numbers at the dissociation limit. The high resolution PA spectroscopy of ultracold atoms has proven to be a valuable method for observation of interesting spectroscopic phenomena. However, further studies are needed for predissociation ($1_g$ and $0_u^+$ states), mixing of rotational and hyperfine interactions ($1_g$) and second-order perturbation of the $0_g^-$ state by the dark $2_u$ state, resulting in vibrational level shifts and observation of additional levels, as well as to extract the true $C_3$ coefficients and the corresponding lifetimes.
